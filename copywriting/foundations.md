# Foundations

Cross-genre principles. Specialized in chapters 02–07; editing in 08.

## What copy does

Three tests. Miss one and the artifact is decorative.

1. Reader understands on first read.
2. Reader can act (buy, click, learn, change mind, feel something specific).
3. Reader does not feel manipulated, condescended to, or bored.

## Audience first

Every draft starts with a one-paragraph brief:

- Who reads this — role, expertise level, vocabulary they use.
- What they already know and don't know about the subject.
- Where they read it — channel, device, mood, time pressure.
- What changes after reading — action, belief, feeling, understanding.

Without the brief, the draft drifts to "the average reader" who doesn't exist (plainlanguage.gov § Write for your audience).

## Four standing principles (Zinsser)

- Clarity — every sentence says exactly what it means.
- Simplicity — short word over long, common over rare, concrete over abstract.
- Brevity — every word does work; cut the rest. First drafts lose 20–50% in editing.
- Humanity — a person wrote this for a person.

Application differs by register and genre; the four are non-negotiable.

## Curse of knowledge

Most bad prose is written by experts who forgot what it was like not to know (Pinker, 2014). Counters:

- Define every domain term on first use, in plain language, in context.
- Show the example before stating the rule.
- Read the draft as the briefed audience.
- Have an outsider read it back.

## Concrete over abstract

Every editing pass replaces abstract nouns with concrete ones, abstract verbs with specific actions, category words with named things.

- "Communicate effectively with stakeholders" → "Send the engineering lead a one-paragraph status by Friday."
- "Significant performance improvements" → "Page load drops from 4.2 s to 1.1 s."
- "A robust solution" → "Runs unattended for 90 days under 5,000 req/s."

Hemingway-style readability tools flag this mechanically. Target grade 7–10 for general audiences.

## Active voice default (Strunk & White Rule 14)

Active names who did what; passive buries the actor. Use passive when:

- Actor is unknown ("the window was broken sometime overnight").
- Actor is irrelevant ("the samples were stored at –20 °C").
- Reader's attention belongs on the object — methods sections, some legal/policy.

Otherwise active wins. See `research-and-academic.md` for academic exceptions.

## Sentence rhythm

Burstiness — variation in sentence length — is the most reliable structural human-vs-AI signal. Targets:

- Average 14–18 words; cap 25.
- One short punch (4–9 words) per paragraph.
- Occasional 28–34-word sentence when the idea earns it.

A piece where every sentence is 18 words reads flat regardless of content.

## Read aloud

Single most reliable edit. The mouth catches what the eye skips: dropped articles, mismatched tenses, clattering consonants, broken transitions.

## Honesty about the form

- A sales page is a sales page; don't disguise it as an article.
- Opinion is opinion; don't pose as settled fact.
- Sponsored content gets labelled.
- Hedge real uncertainty; do not hedge to soften unsupported claims.

Readers forgive a strong declared point of view faster than a piece that pretends neutrality while taking a side.

## The reader is not at fault

If a piece is unclear, the writer is wrong. Build for the reader who scans, skips, leaves, misreads the favourite paragraph.
