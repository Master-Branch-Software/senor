# Register and Formality

Register is the level of formality the piece performs. Pick before the first sentence; hold every dial.

## Five registers (Joos, 1962)

| Register     | Where                                                                                               | Markers                                                                                  |
| ------------ | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Frozen       | Constitutions, prayers, ceremonies, classical poetry                                                | Fixed wording, archaic forms, no contractions, no second person                          |
| Formal       | Academic papers, legal contracts, government reports                                                | Long sentences, Latinate vocabulary, no contractions, third-person dominant, hedged      |
| Consultative | Most professional writing, business email, technical docs, journalism, customer support             | Plain + domain mix, mostly active, occasional contractions, second person common         |
| Casual       | Newsletters with personality, opinion blogs, marketing email to existing customers, podcast scripts | Standard contractions, fragments allowed, idioms permitted, first/second person dominant |
| Intimate     | Notes between close friends, private DMs, fiction in tight first-person                             | Private vocabulary, ellipsis, deep idiom, shared references unexplained                  |

Most professional copy is consultative. Marketing tilts casual. Legal/regulatory/academic tilts formal. Frozen is rare; intimate is fiction-leaning or insider-cultivating brand voices.

## Register markers

Every register sets a position on each dial. A draft is "in register" only when every dial is consistent.

| Marker              | Formal                                                  | Consultative                                                                      | Casual                                                                         |
| ------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Contractions        | None                                                    | Selective (`don't` in conversational sections; `do not` in instructions/warnings) | Standard everywhere                                                            |
| Vocabulary          | Latinate (`commence`, `utilize`, `obtain`)              | Mixed; Anglo-Saxon verbs, Latinate where precision earns it                       | Anglo-Saxon (`start`, `use`, `get`); idiom welcome                             |
| Avg sentence length | 22–28 words                                             | 14–20; cap 25                                                                     | 10–16; fragments allowed                                                       |
| Pronouns            | First-plural rare; second avoided                       | First-plural, second common                                                       | First-singular, second standard                                                |
| Voice               | Passive acceptable, esp. methods                        | Active default                                                                    | Active almost only                                                             |
| Punctuation         | Conservative; semicolons; no exclamation outside quotes | Standard; em dashes for asides                                                    | Em dashes, ellipses, occasional exclamation, parens, channel-appropriate emoji |
| Hedging             | Heavy explicit (`results suggest`)                      | Light modal (`might`, `could`, `usually`)                                         | Direct claims with personal accountability                                     |
| Idiom               | Avoided                                                 | Sparingly; one strong metaphor per section                                        | Welcomed; mixed registers within paragraph for effect                          |

A draft that contracts in some sentences and not others, or mixes verb tiers at random, reads careless regardless of register.

## Anglo-Saxon vs Latinate verbs

Single fastest register lever. Default Anglo-Saxon; reach Latinate only when precision changes meaning (clinical `utilize`, legal `acquire`).

| Anglo-Saxon | Latinate                 |
| ----------- | ------------------------ |
| begin       | commence, initiate       |
| use         | utilize, employ          |
| help        | assist, facilitate       |
| get         | obtain, acquire, procure |
| show        | demonstrate, illustrate  |
| about       | approximately            |
| try         | attempt, endeavor        |
| end         | terminate, conclude      |
| ask         | inquire, request         |
| think       | consider, contemplate    |
| build       | construct, fabricate     |
| break       | fracture, rupture        |
| buy         | purchase                 |
| send        | transmit, dispatch       |

## Picking register

Match register to context, not ambition. Three factors:

1. **Reader's expectation.** Compliance notice in casual register reads suspicious; startup launch in formal register reads embalmed.
2. **Channel.** Print legal, peer-reviewed journals, court filings → formal. Most business/technical/news/customer-facing → consultative. Newsletters with named author, marketing campaigns, scripts, social → casual.
3. **Relationship.** Cold outreach is more formal than communication with existing customers. The closer the prior relationship, the lower the tolerated register.

Wrong register signals contempt or distance even when the words are accurate.

## Switching register within a piece

Allowed when controlled.

- Switch on paragraph break or quoted material, never mid-sentence.
- Mark the first switch ("Here is what the policy actually says, in its words: …").
- Switch back deliberately, not by drift.
- No more than two registers in a piece outside fiction.

## Common failures

- **Mid-formal mush.** LLM default. 18-word sentences, no contractions, Latinate verbs, hedged claims, no second person. Fix: pick a register; rewrite to its markers.
- **Casual on formal content.** Compliance content in casual register reads unserious. Fix: shift up; keep voice through tone, not register.
- **Formal on casual content.** Welcome email or 404 page in formal register. Fix: shift down.
- **Drift.** Hey-there opening, consultative middle, formal close. Fix: pick at brief stage; hold through every pass.
- **Inverted hierarchy.** Casual headlines, formal body. Headlines should be the most accessible part of any piece.

## Default by genre when brief is silent

- Research, legal, regulatory, policy → formal.
- Technical docs, business communication, journalism, customer support, education → consultative.
- Brand newsletters, opinion blogs, marketing, social, scripts → casual.
- Personal essay, fiction, intimate-channel fan content → casual to intimate.

Confirm in writing before drafting; the wrong register is the most expensive mistake to fix in revision.

## Worked example — three registers, same content

Formal:

> The authors recommend that organizations subject to GDPR Article 30 maintain documented records of all processing activities, including purposes, categories of data subjects, and retention periods. Failure to maintain such records may result in administrative fines under Article 83.

Consultative:

> If you process personal data and GDPR applies, Article 30 requires you to keep written records of what you process, why, whose data it is, and how long you keep it. Skipping this exposes the organization to fines under Article 83.

Casual:

> GDPR Article 30 says you have to write down what personal data you process and why. We'd say "obviously," but it's the part most teams skip — and the fines under Article 83 are real.
