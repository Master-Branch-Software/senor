# Sources — 07 Research and Academic Writing

## Primary citations

- Wikipedia, "IMRAD." <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IMRAD>. Source for the dominant structure of empirical research papers (Introduction, Methods, Results, Discussion).
- University of Minnesota Libraries, "Structure of a Research Paper: IMRaD Format." <https://libguides.umn.edu/StructureResearchPaper>. Source for section-by-section conventions.
- George Mason University Writing Center, "Scientific (IMRaD) Research Reports." <https://writingcenter.gmu.edu/writing-resources/imrad/writing-an-imrad-report>. Source for introduction and methods conventions.
- Allan Gaw, "Writing an Effective Abstract: A Study Guide" (University of Edinburgh, 2014). <https://www.docs.hss.ed.ac.uk/iad/Researchers/Research_staff/Study_Guide_How_to_Write_an_Effective_Abstract_v2.0_.pdf>. Source for the structured abstract (Background, Methods, Results, Conclusions).
- American Psychological Association, _Publication Manual of the American Psychological Association_ (7th ed., APA, 2020). Source for the APA citation style and the modern preference for active voice with first-person plural in academic writing.
- "How to write a good abstract for a scientific paper," PMC. <https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3136027/>. Source for typical abstract length (200–250 words) and structure.
- Edward Tufte, _The Visual Display of Quantitative Information_ (Graphics Press, 2nd ed., 2001). Source for figure design principles (avoid 3D bar charts, prefer length over angle encoding).

## Notes for editors

The IMRaD structure dominates empirical research; humanities and theoretical fields use different structures (see chapter body). When extending, cite the field-specific style guide (e.g., MLA Handbook for humanities, AMS for math) rather than generalizing.

The plain-language summary section anticipates funder requirements (NIH, Wellcome, etc.) that have become standard since 2020; cite the specific funder requirement when documenting compliance content.

Hedging guidance in this chapter overlaps with `editing-and-anti-patterns.md` § Hedging verbs as clause padding. The distinction is subtle but real: academic hedging is calibrated to evidence; AI-default hedging hides the absence of evidence. Cross-reference rather than restate.
