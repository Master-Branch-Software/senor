# Voice and Perspective

Voice = who is speaking. Perspective = where they stand. Pick both before drafting.

Covers POV, persona writing (deliberately constructed narrator), and the most common AI-assisted writing failure: stripping the human out to sound "professional."

## POV at a glance

| POV              | Pronouns                    | Best for                                                       | Risks                                    |
| ---------------- | --------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------- |
| First singular   | I, me, my                   | Personal essay, op-ed, memoir, founder letter, expert blog     | Self-indulgence; writer becomes subject  |
| First plural     | we, us                      | Brand voice, organizational, co-authored, manifesto            | Vague antecedent; royal-we condescension |
| Second           | you, your                   | Instructions, tutorials, ad copy, self-help, immersive fiction | Pushy; presumptuous                      |
| Third limited    | she/he/they (one viewpoint) | Profile journalism, case studies, fiction                      | Limited camera                           |
| Third omniscient | she/he/they (any viewpoint) | Sweeping fiction, encyclopedic nonfiction, news                | Distance; clinical                       |
| Third objective  | she/he/they (no interior)   | Hard news, scientific reporting, court documents               | Reads dry by design                      |

Mid-piece switches are rewrites.

## First person

**Singular** — writer is participant; reader sees what writer saw, knows what writer knew.

Works for: memoir, personal essay, opinion (accountability visible), founder letters, expert posts where experience is evidence.

Backfires for: reference docs (reader doesn't care who wrote it), academic work in third-person fields, customer-facing instructions (centers writer, not reader).

**Plural** — two cases:

1. **Organizational we.** "At Stripe, we've found …" — antecedent must be clear.
2. **Inclusive we.** "We've all written code we couldn't read." — earns intimacy; backfires if shared experience is not real. Inclusive `we` from experts to novices reads as condescending.

## Second person

Strongest verb-driven POV. Every sentence is implicit instruction or invitation. Default for tutorials, product copy, ad scripts, self-help, modern marketing. Google + Microsoft developer style guides standardize it.

Why instruction: reader is the actor. "You install the package" reads as a script the reader follows.
Why marketing: implies relationship. "You'll save four hours a week" is a promise.

Failure modes:

- **Pushy.** "You need to act now or you'll regret it." — manipulative.
- **Presumptuous.** "You probably struggle with imposter syndrome." — projection.
- **Empty.** "You can use this tool to do many things." — `you` carries no specificity.

## Third person

Default for journalism, much academic writing, biography, fiction beyond intimate first.

- **Limited** — camera follows one perspective.
- **Omniscient** — camera moves freely.
- **Objective** — actions and dialogue, no interior states.

Harder to keep alive than first/second; compensate with strong scene-setting, characters, verbs, named details. Common failure: drifts into objective when interior detail runs out — second half loses quotes, scenes, named perspective.

## Persona writing

A persona is a constructed narrator distinct from the writer — name, stance, vocabulary, idiosyncratic patterns, defined relationship to the subject.

Heart of fiction; also operates in nonfiction (mascot voices, advice columns, fan-club newsletters, satirical commentary, ghost-written exec content).

### "I am a cat" — canonical example

Sōseki's _Wagahai wa Neko de Aru_ (1905). Narrator is a stray cat speaking in `wagahai` — high-register first-person pronoun fit for a samurai. Mismatch between speaker and register is the point.

Lesson: persona = (subject) × (register) × (relationship to subject). Change any of the three and the persona changes. Cat in casual register = children's book; cat in formal register = satire; cat in intimate register = fable.

Deeper lesson: persona is signaled by **sustained idiosyncrasy**, not one-off quirks. Sōseki holds the cat's `wagahai` 600 pages.

### Six-dial persona sheet

Set, then hold:

1. **Identity.** Who speaks (specifically). "Friendly expert" is not a persona.
2. **Register.** See `register-and-formality.md`. Persona register can differ from publication's typical register; that mismatch is often the point.
3. **Vocabulary fingerprint.** Three to five words the persona overuses; three to five they will not say.
4. **Sentence shape.** Long balanced (Sage), short punchy (Hero), fragmentary (Outlaw), digressive (Jester). See `front-end/brand-and-copywriting.md` § 12 archetypes.
5. **Stance.** What the persona believes, what annoys them, what surprises them. No strong opinions → no voice.
6. **Relationship to reader.** Friend, mentor, peer, antagonist, witness, host. Determines second-person usage, hedging density, joke license.

### Persona failures

- **Sounds like the writer.** Default failure when dial work is skipped.
- **Costume, not character.** Quirky vocabulary without coherent stance.
- **Unstable.** First chapter pet-store-owner; second chapter philosophy professor.
- **Condescends.** Translator persona that explains things in baby talk.
- **Ignores the brief.** Same persona for annual report and Instagram caption.

## Don't strip the human

Most common AI-assisted failure: writer accepts the model's default — neutral, unattributed, mid-formal, no idiosyncrasy. Technically grammatical and unreadable.

Marks of a stripped-out voice:

- No contractions where register expects them.
- No first or second person where genre allows.
- No specific anecdote, named example, dated reference.
- No opinion strong enough to invite disagreement.
- No sentence the writer would feel embarrassed _or_ proud to read aloud.

Counters:

- Add a named anecdote only this writer could have written.
- State an opinion that splits the room ("Most standups are theater" beats "Effective communication is essential").
- Use the contractions the register allows. Absence is the strongest AI fingerprint.
- Keep the writer's idiom, not the model's.
- Allow imperfect rhythm. A piece where every sentence flows is suspicious; a sentence that lands awkwardly because it's true is human.

Same lesson as Sōseki: a persona's signal is sustained idiosyncrasy. Strip it and the writer disappears.

## Voice consistency check

Run before submitting:

1. POV matches brief and is stable first-to-last sentence?
2. Pronouns consistent? (No drift `I`↔`we`; no drift between reader-`you` and generic-`you`.)
3. Vocabulary fingerprint shows up in every section?
4. Stance shows up in every section? (≥1 opinion or named preference per major section.)
5. First and last paragraph sound like the same person?
6. Read aloud — where the voice flattens, that section needs another pass.
